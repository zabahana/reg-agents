# OCC 2011-12 — Sound Practices for Model Risk Management

## Relationship to SR 11-7
OCC Bulletin 2011-12 and Federal Reserve SR 11-7 share identical supervisory
text on model risk management. National banks and federal savings associations
are examined against these expectations. The guidance is principles-based and
scales with the bank's size, complexity, and model use.

## Definition of a Model
A model consists of three components: an information input component that
delivers data and assumptions; a processing component that transforms inputs
into estimates; and a reporting component that translates the estimates into
useful business information. Deficiencies in any component create model risk.

## Sources of Model Risk
Model risk occurs primarily for two reasons: (1) a model may have fundamental
errors and produce inaccurate outputs when viewed against its design objective;
and (2) a model may be used incorrectly or inappropriately, or its limitations
and assumptions may be poorly understood.

## Model Development Standards
A sound development process articulates the model purpose, design, theory, and
logic; uses sound statistical and mathematical techniques; documents data
sources and processing; and confirms outputs against business objectives.
Developers should test model behavior over a range of input values, including
stress conditions and boundary cases.

## Model Implementation Controls
Controls should verify that a model is implemented as designed, including code
review, reconciliation of the production system to the specification, and
change management. Access controls and version control reduce operational risk.

## Model Use and Reliance
Business users should understand model limitations and avoid over-reliance.
Where models inform high-impact decisions, compensating controls, overrides,
and human review should be documented, with override rates monitored.

## Validation Independence
Validation should be performed by staff with appropriate incentives,
competence, and influence, and who are independent of model development and
use. Where full independence is impractical, compensating controls and senior
review should be documented.

## Benchmarking
Benchmarking compares a model's inputs and outputs to alternative data,
estimates, or challenger models. Material differences should be investigated
and explained.

## Governance, Policies, and Controls
The board and senior management are responsible for establishing a model risk
management framework, an inventory of all models with risk ratings, clear
roles and responsibilities, and policies covering development, validation, use,
and retirement of models.

## Vendor Models
For third-party models, banks should validate conceptual soundness to the
extent possible, monitor performance, and maintain contingency plans for vendor
failure. Banks remain fully responsible for the appropriate use of vendor
models.
