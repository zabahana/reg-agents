## 2 · Assessment narrative

The two-stage complaint classification model under review leverages a combination of traditional machine learning and retrieval-augmented generation techniques to classify consumer complaints into various regulation categories. The model's conceptual soundness is a critical aspect of its overall effectiveness, and this report will assess its fitness for purpose.

The two-stage design, comprising a stage-1 TF-IDF classifier and a stage-2 retrieval-augmented generation pipeline (rag_llm), is a reasonable approach to tackle the complexity of the task. The stage-1 models, logistic_regression and xgboost, demonstrate competitive performance on the primary metric, pr_auc, with values of 0.992 and 0.9896, respectively. However, the stage-2 rag_llm pipeline, which relies on the NeMo Retriever embeddings and the LLM labeler, exhibits lower accuracy (0.28) and family accuracy (0.41), indicating potential limitations in its ability to generalize and capture nuanced relationships between complaints and regulation categories.

One critical assumption underlying the model's design is the use of weak supervision, where labels are derived from the CFPB product/issue taxonomy and narrative keyword rules. This approach is inherently limited, as it may not accurately capture the complexity and subtlety of human-adjudicated labels. The reliance on weak supervision is flagged as a limitation in the validation report, and its impact on downstream metrics, including the distinction between measured agreement and true accuracy, warrants careful consideration.

The outcomes analysis reveals some concerning trends. The stage-1 models exhibit high pr_auc values, but the stage-2 rag_llm pipeline struggles to achieve even moderate accuracy. The family accuracy (0.41) is lower than the exact agreement (0.28), indicating that the model is more likely to agree with itself than with the true labels. This discrepancy may be attributed to the weak supervision approach and the potential for overfitting in the stage-2 pipeline.

Furthermore, the outcomes analysis highlights small-support instability, where the model's performance varies significantly across different regulation categories. This instability is a concern, as it may lead to biased or inaccurate classification decisions. The recall values for some categories, such as FDCPA_THREATS (0.0) and TILA_ORIGINATION (0.0), are particularly concerning, indicating that the model is unable to capture relevant relationships between complaints and regulation categories.

In terms of monitoring and guardrails adequacy, the report notes that the model's performance is not adequately monitored, and there are no clear guardrails in place to prevent overfitting or biased classification decisions. This lack of oversight is a critical concern, as it may lead to unintended consequences, such as inaccurate classification decisions or biased treatment of certain regulation categories.

Finally, the report highlights the potential consumer-protection/fair-lending exposure of complaint routing. The model's classification decisions may have a direct impact on the treatment of consumer complaints, and any biases or inaccuracies in the model may lead to unfair treatment of certain consumers. This exposure warrants careful consideration and mitigation strategies to ensure that the model is fair, transparent, and accountable.

Based on the findings outlined above, the report identifies the following validation findings:

V-1: **High** - The two-stage design, while conceptually sound, may be vulnerable to overfitting and biased classification decisions due to the reliance on weak supervision and the potential for small-support instability. Remediation: Implement stronger supervision techniques, such as human-adjudicated labels, and monitor the model's performance across different regulation categories. Owner: Model Development Team.

V-2: **Medium** - The stage-2 rag_llm pipeline exhibits lower accuracy and family accuracy, indicating potential limitations in its ability to generalize and capture nuanced relationships between complaints and regulation categories. Remediation: Investigate alternative stage-2 pipelines or techniques that can improve the model's accuracy and family accuracy. Owner: Model Development Team.

V-3: **Low** - The model's performance is not adequately monitored, and there are no clear guardrails in place to prevent overfitting or biased classification decisions. Remediation: Implement monitoring and guardrails to ensure that the model's performance is transparent and accountable. Owner: Model Operations Team.

V-4: **High** - The model's classification decisions may have a direct impact on the treatment of consumer complaints, and any biases or inaccuracies in the model may lead to unfair treatment of certain consumers. Remediation: Implement mitigation strategies to ensure that the model is fair, transparent, and accountable. Owner: Model Development Team.

Based on these findings, the disposition of this report is:

Approve with Conditions:

1. Implement stronger supervision techniques, such as human-adjudicated labels, to mitigate the impact of weak supervision.
2. Investigate alternative stage-2 pipelines or techniques that can improve the model's accuracy and family accuracy.
3. Implement monitoring and guardrails to ensure that the model's performance is transparent and accountable.
4. Implement mitigation strategies to ensure that the model is fair, transparent, and accountable.

By addressing these conditions, the model can be improved to ensure that it is accurate, fair, and accountable, and that it meets the regulatory requirements for complaint classification.

## 4 · Key limitations (validator-confirmed)

1. **Weak labels** — ground truth derives from the CFPB issue taxonomy +
   keyword rules, not human adjudication; stage-2 "accuracy" is agreement with
   a noisy reference. Condition: human-adjudicated golden set (≥25/category).
2. **Class imbalance** — several categories (SCRA/MLA, sales practices,
   adverse action) have thin support; per-category metrics are unstable there.
3. **LLM nondeterminism & drift** — prompt/model versions must be pinned and
   re-evaluated on the golden set before any provider/model change (the system
   records provider + model in every artifact banner).
4. **Fair-lending lens** — complaint routing affects remediation speed;
   monitoring should include parity of routing outcomes across products.

## 5 · Disposition

**Approve with Conditions** — see conditions in the assessment narrative and
limitations above; re-validation triggered by model/prompt change or stage-1
PSI > 0.25.
