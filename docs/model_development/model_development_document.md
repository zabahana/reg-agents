## Abstract

This complaint-classification model is designed to address a binary regulatory gate problem, where the majority of consumer-complaint narratives are classified as regulatory in nature, while a smaller minority are non-regulatory. The model development protocol involved stratifying the data into training, validation, and testing sets, with a 5% scoring holdout reserved first, followed by an 80/10/10 split. Four minority-balanced candidates were evaluated, and the champion model was selected based on its performance on the validation minority PR-AUC metric. The champion model, logistic_regression, achieved a minority PR-AUC of 0.7258 on the validation fold and 0.642 on the test fold, with a decision cut-off optimized to 0.397. Notably, the champion model deployed at this cut-off achieved a test minority F1 score of 0.7009. However, it is essential to acknowledge two caveats that impact the model's performance: the weak labels used in the training data and the small-minority variance quantified by seed stability, which resulted in a test minority PR-AUC of 0.6697 ± 0.0327.

## 1 · Introduction and objective

CMPL-REG-24 routes consumer complaints in two stages: a cheap, high-recall
**stage-1 gate** decides whether a narrative has any regulatory nexus, and
only gated-in complaints reach the expensive stage-2 RAG+LLM labeler that
assigns one of 24 regulation categories with citations. This document covers
the development of the stage-1 gate: data understanding, experimental
design, candidate estimation, selection, and robustness evidence. The gate
is Tier-2 (medium risk): a false negative delays a regulatory complaint into
the standard service queue; a false positive costs one unnecessary LLM call.
That asymmetry, and the extreme class imbalance documented below, drive
every methodological choice that follows.

## 4 · Experimental design

**Split.** A stratified 5% scoring holdout is reserved FIRST — it feeds the
batch-ingestion layer (`scripts/score_batch.py`, UI upload) and is never
touched by training, validation, test, or threshold tuning. The remaining
95% is split stratified 80/10/10 train/validation/test on `is_regulatory`,
fixed seed 42 (Table 5). The test fold is split off first and touched
exactly once — to produce the final columns of Table 7. Exact fold
membership is committed in [`artifacts/split_indices.json`](artifacts/split_indices.json).

**Table 5 — Split composition.**

| split | rows | regulatory | non-regulatory | reg rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| train (80%) | 3,040 | 2,660 | 380 | 87.50% |
| validation (10%) | 380 | 333 | 47 | 87.63% |
| test (10%) | 380 | 332 | 48 | 87.37% |
| scoring holdout (5% of full) | 200 | 175 | 25 | 87.50% |

**Balancing.** Every candidate up-weights the minority class:
`class_weight='balanced'` (logistic regression, LightGBM),
`scale_pos_weight=0.1` (XGBoost), class-weighted cross-entropy
(DistilBERT). Section 8 ablates this choice.

**Selection metric.** Minority-class PR-AUC on the **validation** fold.
With 87.5% of complaints regulatory, majority PR-AUC saturates near 1.0 and
accuracy is uninformative; the minority PR-AUC is where candidates actually
differ (Davis & Goadrich 2006; Saito & Rehmsmeier 2015).

**Decision cut-off.** The default 0.5 threshold is never assumed. Each
candidate's cut-off on P(regulatory) is optimized on the **validation** fold
by maximizing minority-class F1 (Table 7, "cut-off" column); the champion
deploys at **0.397**. The test fold plays no role in the choice,
and Figure 7 shows the full precision/recall/F1 trade-off around it.

## 9 · Discussion and model selection decision

The champion model, logistic_regression, was selected based on its superior performance on the validation fold, achieving a minority-class PR-AUC of 0.7258. This metric was chosen as the selection criterion, as it is a key performance indicator for models tasked with classifying complaints into regulatory and non-regulatory categories. The test fold was used only once for final reporting, ensuring that the model's performance was not over-optimized for the test set.

The validation champion's performance on the test fold was also notable, with a minority-class PR-AUC of 0.642 and a minority F1 score of 0.7009. This suggests that the model's ability to classify complaints correctly is robust across different data folds. However, the test minority PR-AUC of 0.642 is lower than the validation minority PR-AUC of 0.7258, indicating a potential validation-test gap. This gap may be attributed to small-minority variance, which can result from the limited number of non-regulatory complaints in the dataset (KEY NUMBERS: {"class_balance": {"regulatory": "3,500 (87.5%)", "non_regulatory_minority": "500 (12.5%)"}}).

The seed-stability band, which measures the standard deviation of the minority PR-AUC across different random seeds, is relatively narrow on both the validation and test folds (KEY NUMBERS: {"seed_stability_minority_pr_auc": {"validation_mean_and_std": "0.7119 ± 0.0304", "test_mean_and_std": "0.6697 ± 0.0327"}}). This suggests that the model's performance is relatively stable across different random seeds.

The perturbation results, which examine the impact of different data preprocessing techniques on the model's performance, are also informative. The removal of CFPB 'XXXX' PII masks and the use of lowercase + strip punctuation both result in a modest improvement in the minority PR-AUC, while the removal of the first 50% of narrative tokens and the random dropping of 10% of tokens result in a significant decrease in performance. This suggests that the model is sensitive to the presence of certain types of data, particularly those related to PII and narrative text.

The class-weight ablation results, which examine the impact of different class weights on the model's performance, are also notable. The use of unweighted class weights results in a modest decrease in performance on the validation fold, while the use of balanced class weights (deployed in the champion model) results in a significant improvement in performance.

The decision cut-off for the champion model was optimized on the validation fold by maximizing the minority F1 score, rather than using the default cut-off of 0.5. This resulted in a cut-off of 0.397, which is lower than the default cut-off. This decision was made to optimize the model's performance on the minority class, which is the class of interest in this problem.

Finally, the cost of using BERT, which achieved a minority PR-AUC of 0.5759, is not justified by its performance. While BERT's performance is respectable, it is not significantly better than the champion model's performance, and its fit time is much longer (KEY NUMBERS: {"models": {"bert_finetuned": {"val_minority_pr_auc": 0.5759, "fit_time_seconds_not_a_metric": 97.9}}}).

Based on these results, we recommend deploying the logistic_regression model, which achieved the best performance on the validation fold and has a relatively stable performance across different random seeds. We also recommend revisiting the decision to deploy this model if the validation-test gap widens or if the model's performance on the minority class degrades significantly.

## 10 · Limitations and monitoring

1. **Weak labels.** All metrics measure agreement with CFPB-taxonomy weak
   supervision; a human-adjudicated golden set is a standing validation
   condition before agreement can be read as accuracy.
2. **Minority support.** 500 minority cases overall (~47 per
   held-out fold under 80/10/10) put wide bands on minority metrics
   (Table 13) and make the tuned cut-off itself an estimate; both are
   re-checked at every retrain, and champion/challenger gaps inside the
   band are not decision-grade on their own.
3. **Snapshot vocabulary.** OOV exposure is modest today (Table 9) but the
   complaint lexicon drifts; PSI-based drift monitoring on score and
   token-coverage distributions feeds the existing Prometheus/Grafana
   guardrail stack, with alerting via Alertmanager.
4. **Truncation fragility.** Table 11 shows truncating narratives to 50%
   costs the most PR-AUC of any tested perturbation; ingestion must deliver
   full narratives.
