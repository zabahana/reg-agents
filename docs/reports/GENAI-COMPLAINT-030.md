## Appendix A — Validation Agent findings

### Model Validation Findings for GenAI Complaint Classification & Regulatory Mapping (GENAI-COMPLAINT-030)

#### 1. Conceptual Soundness
**GAP:** The model's reliance on a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) approach introduces potential risks related to hallucination, especially with ambiguous complaints. While the documentation mentions mitigation strategies, it lacks a detailed framework for assessing the impact of these risks on classification accuracy and regulatory mapping.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Develop a formal risk assessment framework that quantifies the impact of hallucination on model outputs. This should include a systematic evaluation of ambiguous complaint scenarios and their effects on regulatory compliance.

#### 2. Data Quality
**GAP:** The model uses a historical dataset of 120k labeled complaints, but there is no mention of the data's recency or the procedures in place to ensure ongoing data quality. The documentation notes that the regulation corpus must be kept current, yet it lacks a clear process for regular updates and validation of the training data.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Implement a data governance framework that includes regular audits of the training dataset for recency and accuracy. Establish a procedure for updating the regulation corpus and validating the quality of incoming complaint data to ensure maximum possible accuracy as mandated by FCRA [FCRA, Accuracy and Data Quality].

#### 3. Testing/Outcomes Analysis
**GAP:** The model's performance metrics (Macro-F1 0.88 and retrieval hit-rate@5 0.93) are promising, but the documentation does not provide details on the testing methodology or the representativeness of the held-out sample. Additionally, the qualitative evaluation of explanation faithfulness lacks a quantitative counterpart.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Enhance the testing framework by providing detailed methodologies for performance evaluation, including the representativeness of the test set. Introduce quantitative metrics for explanation faithfulness to align with best practices in model explainability [UDAAP, Model Explainability for Consumer-Facing Decisions].

#### 4. Ongoing Monitoring
**GAP:** While the model includes weekly monitoring of F1 scores and drift analysis, there is insufficient detail on how these metrics are used to inform model adjustments or governance decisions. The monitoring process appears reactive rather than proactive.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Establish a more robust ongoing monitoring framework that includes predefined thresholds for performance metrics and a structured process for model recalibration based on monitoring outcomes. This should align with the requirements for transaction monitoring systems [FFIEC, Transaction Monitoring Systems].

#### 5. Fair-Lending/ECOA Exposure
**GAP:** The model does not explicitly address the potential for bias in classification outcomes, particularly given that it processes free-text complaints without defined protected class features. There is no mention of fair-lending reviews or assessments of proxy variables that could lead to discriminatory outcomes.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Conduct a comprehensive fair-lending review to assess the model's potential for bias, especially in how it classifies complaints related to protected classes. Implement feature governance practices to ensure that no features inadvertently act as proxies for protected classes, as outlined in ECOA regulations [ECOA, Model Feature Governance].

### Summary
The GenAI Complaint Classification & Regulatory Mapping model demonstrates a solid foundation for classifying consumer complaints and mapping them to regulations. However, addressing the identified gaps through the recommended actions will enhance its conceptual soundness, data quality, testing rigor, ongoing monitoring, and compliance with fair-lending regulations.
